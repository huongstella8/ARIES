Topic 1: Media, Fame & Bias

Use these words to discuss how news is reported, the celebrity world, and media fairness.

📌 Part A — The World of Journalism Core Vocabulary
The press / journalism
The profession of collecting, writing and reporting news
A daily paper
A newspaper published every day except Sunday
Current affairs
Important political or social events happening right now
Publish an article
To prepare and release a news story in print or online
A rolling news edition
A continuous, constantly-updated online or TV news stream
Breaking news
An important story that has just happened — e.g. "Breaking news: earthquake hits the capital"
📌 Part B — Decoding Dramatic Headlines Headline Language
🧠 Memory Hack — "Same Form" Rule: Many headline words work as both a verb AND a noun. Learn both uses at once!
Clash
To fight or disagree seriously (V) / A serious conflict (N) — "Police clash with protesters" / "a violent clash"
Axe
To cut a large number of jobs (V) — "200 workers axed by factory"
Pledge / Promise
To make a formal public promise (V) / The promise itself (N) — "Government pledges aid to victims"
Claim
To state something is true, without proof (V) / The statement (N) — "He claims it is true" / "He made a claim"
Aid
Money, food, or help sent to people in need (N/V) — "Government pledges aid to flood victims"
Scare
A situation causing public fear or worry (N) — "a health scare / a bomb scare"
Demonstrate / Protest
To show public disagreement (V) / A public march (N) — "Thousands protest in the streets"

📌 Part D — Media Bias: Opposites to Know Describing Journalists
❌ Unfair
Biased / Prejudiced / Subjective
Unfairly supporting only one side; not fair to all views
"The report was clearly biased against the government."
✅ Fair
Impartial / Objective
Fair and balanced; presenting all sides without taking a side
"Good journalists try to remain impartial at all times."
Sensational
Making stories seem more shocking than they are to attract readers
Influential
Having a powerful effect on people's opinions and decisions

Topic 2: Natural Disasters

Describe dramatic events step-by-step: The Event → The Damage → The Response

📌 Part A — The Disasters (Types) Nouns
Earthquake
A sudden violent movement of the Earth's crust
Hurricane / Typhoon
A violent tropical storm with extremely strong circular winds
Volcanic eruption
When a volcano explodes and throws out hot lava and ash
Drought
A long period without rain causing crops and water supplies to fail
Tidal wave / Tsunami
A massive ocean wave caused by underwater earthquakes
Flood
A large amount of water covering land that is normally dry

📌 Part C — The Disaster Timeline: Event → Damage → Response
🔴 Phase 1: The Event
struck / hit — The earthquake struck the coastal city. (= sudden impact)
exploded / went off — A volcano erupted without warning. (= sudden release of energy)
🟠 Phase 2: The Damage
collapsed — Buildings collapsed to the ground. (= fell completely)
were trapped — Many people were trapped under the rubble. (= stuck, unable to escape)
rising death toll — There was a rising death toll of over 500. (= increasing number of deaths)
starve to death — Villagers could starve to death without food supplies. (= die from hunger)
🟢 Phase 3: The Response
evacuate — Emergency teams evacuated 10,000 residents. (= moved people to safety)
rescue — Firefighters rescued survivors from the rubble. (= saved people)
maintain supplies — Aid workers maintained supplies of clean water. (= kept providing essentials)

Topic 3: Crime & The Justice System

Learn crimes, criminals, and the justice process as a complete word family matrix.

📌 Part A — Crime & Criminal Matrix Word Families
🧠 Memory Hack — Steal vs. Rob: You steal things (money, a bike, a phone), but you rob people or places (a bank, a museum, a person). "The thief stole his wallet" ✔ / "The robber robbed the bank" ✔
The Crime (Noun) The Criminal (Person) The Action (Verb + Example)
Theft (taking property) Thief Steals — "steals cars / money / phones"
Robbery (theft using violence) Robber Robs — "robs a bank / robs a person"
Burglary (entering building to steal) Burglar Breaks into — "breaks into a house and steals"
Shoplifting (stealing from a shop) Shoplifter Shoplifts — "steals from a supermarket"
Mugging (attacking in public to steal) Mugger Mugs — "mugs someone on a dark street"
Assault (attacking and hurting) Attacker Assaults / Stabs / Attacks
Murder (killing deliberately) Murderer Murders / Shoots / Kills
📌 Part B — The Justice Timeline Step-by-Step

Learn the 6 stages of the justice system in order — this is a common IELTS Speaking Part 3 topic.

A victim (person harmed) or witness (person who saw it) reports the crime to police.
Police investigate the crime scene and look for evidence (fingerprints, CCTV footage, witnesses).
Police catch / arrest a suspect — the person they believe committed the crime.
If there is enough evidence, police charge the suspect, who then appears in court.
In court: The prosecution tries to prove the accused is guilty; the defence argues they are innocent. A judge and jury decide the verdict.
If found guilty, the judge gives a sentence — e.g., a prison term or a fine.

Topic 4: Politics, War & Peace

Learn government vocabulary, conflict language, and verb→noun word families for academic speech.

📌 Part A — Government & Politics Core Collocations
Hold an election
When citizens vote to choose their political leaders
Political party
A group of people with shared political beliefs; the party with the majority governs
Announce a policy
To publicly state a new government plan or rule
Launch a campaign
To start a public effort to achieve a goal — e.g., "a health campaign / a fitness campaign"
Face opposition
To deal with groups who strongly disagree with your plans
Focus on a priority
To give all attention and resources to the most important issue
Persuade
To convince someone to agree with you by giving good reasons

📌 Part C — War & Peace: From Start to Finish
💥 The Start
A civil war broke out — a war between groups inside the same country. ("The conflict broke out suddenly.")
A bomb exploded / went off in the capital city.
⚔️ The Conflict
Soldiers fought in battle, trying to capture the enemy (= take control of them).
One side was forced to surrender / give up (= admit defeat).
🕊️ The Peace
Allies (countries supporting each other) stepped in to help.
Both sides agreed to a ceasefire — an agreement to stop all fighting.
Leaders met to negotiate a peace settlement — a formal, lasting end to the war — by choosing to compromise (accept less than they wanted).

📌 Part B — Condition → Adjective Word Families Word Building
Condition (Noun) Person / Adjective Key Collocation

📌 Part C — Health Issues by Age Group
👦 Common in Young People
Asthma / Allergies
Breathing difficulty caused by pollution, food, or dust
Obesity
Being very overweight; can lead to heart attacks & diabetes
Infectious diseases
Illnesses that pass easily from person to person
Depression
A serious medical condition of persistent sadness
👵 Common in Older People
Disability
Not being able to use a part of the body properly
Stroke
A sudden brain illness that can stop movement or speech
Arthritis
Joint pain especially in fingers, knees, and hips
Memory loss
Gradual loss of the ability to remember things
📌 Part D — Key Health Action Verbs Speaking Chunks
suffer from
To have a medical condition over time — "suffer from asthma / depression / memory loss"
lead to
To cause a bad result — "Obesity can lead to diabetes and heart attacks"
go blind / deaf
To lose sight or hearing — "Diabetes can cause patients to go blind"
be diagnosed with
When a doctor confirms you have a condition — "She was diagnosed with arthritis"
